# 3.3 — Media & Content Notes

Content-packaging notes for the lesson **3.3 — Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS)
Baseline Correction**. These are production notes only; the technical content
lives in the notebook.

## Working title

*The Baseline Lie: Why Your Peaks Aren't Where You Think*

## YouTube packaging

- **Thumbnail angle:** the three-panel "same chemistry, three backgrounds" frame
  — identical peaks sitting on flat / sloped / bowed baselines — with a big
  overlaid caption like "SAME sample, 3 different answers." The punch is that the
  band *ratio* changes even though nothing about the chemistry did.
- **First 15 seconds (the hook):** "These two spectra are the exact same sample.
  One says the first peak is twice the second; the other says they're equal. The
  difference is a baseline — and if you don't remove it right, your numbers are
  fiction."

## Teaching arc (why it's ordered this way)

Lead with the *problem*, not the algorithm. The viewer should feel the damage —
height, area, and ratio all shifting — before AsLS is even mentioned. Then build
AsLS from scratch (two ideas: smooth + asymmetric), show the two knobs, and end
on judgment: how to tell a good correction from a bad one *without* an answer key.
That last part is the differentiator from every "here's the function" tutorial.

## Follow-up episode

*Baseline Correction in the Wild: Raman Fluorescence and NIR Scatter* — apply the
same AsLS reasoning to the two backgrounds that hurt most in real spectroscopy,
and compare AsLS to polynomial and rubber-band methods.

## LinkedIn / short-form angle

A single carousel: slide 1 the two "same sample, different ratio" spectra; slides
2–3 the λ and p sweeps; final slide the four-point "is my correction honest?"
checklist. The checklist is the saveable, shareable artifact.
